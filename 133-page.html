Outswinging corners

An outswinger bends away from the goalmouth. The ball travels toward attackers arriving from deeper positions, giving them a cleaner run and often a higher contact point.

Why teams choose it

  • The goalkeeper is pulled away from the goal line rather than attacked directly.
  • A tall centre-back can build speed from the edge of the box.
  • The second phase is easier to organize because the ball exits the six-yard crowd.
  • A disguised short pass can make the outswinger appear at the last moment.

The trade-off

An outswinger is easier to attack on the run—but it is also easier to clear if the timing is late. The taker must beat the first defensive line and land the ball before the runners arrive.

Contact point

Meet the ball in stride

The strongest header is often not the highest jump. A runner who arrives balanced, with eyes on the flight, can direct an outswinging corner down or across goal.
