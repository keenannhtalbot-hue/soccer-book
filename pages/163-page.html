Section XI · Fan culture & history

Ultras around the world

ONE IDEA, MANY TERRACES

The word ultras is strongly associated with Italy, but organized supporter culture now appears in distinct local forms across Europe, North Africa, Latin America, the Middle East, and Asia.

Shared ingredients

  • Europe: curva and tribune groups use choreographies, pyro where legal, drums, and dense away travel.
  • North Africa: ultras became influential in city identity and stadium politics, with elaborate banners and call-and-response songs.
  • Latin America: barras and torcidas combine flags, percussion, giant cloths, and neighbourhood networks.
  • Asia: supporter groups adapt the terrace model to club identity, choreographed clapping, and highly visual displays.

Local language matters

A chant melody may travel from one country to another, but the words, gestures, colours, and rivalries are local. Importing a style never replaces knowing the club’s own story.

A world of terraces

Supporter culture is global in form but local in meaning. The same tools—voice, cloth, paint, movement—produce different identities in different cities.

The terrace is a media channel

A display may last three minutes in the stadium, then circulate for days as a photograph, a short video, and a symbol of the club’s public identity.
