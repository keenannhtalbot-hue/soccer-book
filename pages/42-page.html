Section IV

Centre-back archetypes

The centre-back position has split into at least four distinct archetypes in the modern game. Every successful back line is, essentially, a balanced pairing of these roles — a stopper-sweeper axis, a left-right pairing, or a three in which each role is filled.

The headline trend is the rise of the ball-playing CB: teams that could not build from the back used to concede possession in their own third. Now they don’t — and the ball-playing CB is the player that makes the difference.

The four centre-back archetypes
CB 1–4

Four ways to play the centre of a back line. Each archetype is a different answer to one question: do you push up to the ball, or sit and read it?

Ball-playing CB

Comfortable in possession; splits centre-backs and starts the attack with a 10-yard pass into the #6. Often the centre-back who reads the press and the line. Examples: Virgil van Dijk, Rúben Dias, Aymeric Laporte.

Aggressive stopper

Front-foot defender; steps into the duel, leads with the body, often wins the ball before the striker can receive. Needs a cover-shadow partner. Examples: Sergio Ramos, Thiago Silva, Kalidou Koulibaly.

Cover-shadow CB

Reads play; sits; cleans up after the aggressive stopper’s press. Often slightly smaller, faster, smarter. Examples: Franz Beckenbauer (the original), Gérard Piqué, Matthijs de Ligt.

No-nonsense CB

Old-school: marks the centre-forward, wins the header, clears the line. Rarely crosses the halfway. The late-1980s English-style. Examples: John Terry (early), Marcos Rojo, Bremer.
