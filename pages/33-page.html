The 4-4-2 classic

Two flat banks of four, two strikers up top. The defining shape of the British game — and, with variations, of most European football — for the latter half of the twentieth century.

The 4-4-2 balances the pitch in three zones: four defenders form a flat back line; four midfielders split into pairs (wide and central); two strikers lead the press and the counter-attack. Width comes from the full-backs and the wide midfielders.

Its weakness is structural: against a deep-lying regista and a mobile front four (the 4-2-3-1), the two strikers can be outnumbered in central midfield, and the flat-back-four concedes space between the lines.

Hall of fame

Brazil 1970, AC Milan 1988–90, Manchester United 1998–99, Leicester City 2015–16 — four of the great 4-4-2 sides, separated by three decades and three continents.

Why it endures

Every coach knows where their next pass is going. Players learn the shape from age seven. Recruitment is simple — find a full-back, two centre-backs, four midfielders, two strikers.
