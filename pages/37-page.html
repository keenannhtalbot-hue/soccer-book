Section IV

The 3-4-2-1 false-9 evolution

A back three, a midfield four (wing-backs plus two central midfielders), two #10s tucked behind a false nine — a striker who drops deep to receive and let wing-backs or #10s run in behind.

The false nine is the key. With no traditional number 9 to mark, opposing centre-backs must decide: follow the false nine into midfield (and leave the back line exposed), or hold the line (and let the false nine receive unopposed). Messi, Fàbregas for Spain at Euro 2012, and Haaland at times for City have all played the role.

The shape is a front-five in possession (the two #10s and three centre-backs funnel into passing lanes) and a back-five out of possession (the wing-backs drop to the back four).

Hall of fame

Spain 2012, Barcelona 2009–15, Manchester City 2022–24. Each squeezed through the centre by giving the false nine freedom to roam.

The problem of the #10

Two #10s behind one striker means three players in a 20-metre zone between the lines. Brilliant to play in, hard to defend transitions from — the 3-4-2-1 is vulnerable to a single deep ball over the top into the space vacated by the false nine.

The 3-4-2-1 false 9
3 – 4 – 2 – 1

Three centre-backs, wing-backs, two #10s behind a striker who drops to receive between the lines. The shape that defined Spain 2012, Barcelona under Messi, and Guardiola’s Manchester City.
