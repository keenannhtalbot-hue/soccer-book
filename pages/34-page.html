The 4-3-3 modern standard

A back four, a midfield three, and a front three built around a centre-forward with two wingers. The 4-3-3 is the modern standard — it became dominant after Spain won Euro 2008 and the 2010 World Cup with it.

Its strength is numerical superiority in midfield: three against two (versus a 4-4-2), plus a free eighth that drops into the half-space to create passing triangles. The trade-off is a single striker — that requires a complete number 9.

Variants include the 4-3-3 false 9 (no striker — the centre-forward drops deep), the 4-1-4-1 (defensive six shielding the back four), and the inverted 4-3-3 where the wide forwards tuck inside.

Hall of fame

Ajax 1995 (Louis van Gaal), Barcelona 2008–12 (Guardiola), Spain 2008–12, Liverpool 2017–19 (Klopp). Each played the shape with extreme attacking intent.

The inverted full-back

The defining tactical trick of the modern 4-3-3: when the winger tucks inside, the full-back steps into the wide midfield slot, creating a back-three-with-midfield-three structure that traps the opposition.

4 – 3 – 3

A back four, a midfield three, and a front line of a striker flanked by two wingers. The shape that became the global standard under Guardiola, Klopp, and the Spanish national team.
