IX · THE PLAYERS

Inverted full-back

The inverted full-back (iFB) is the tactical innovation of the 2020s. The full-back's starting position is inside the touchline: when the team has the ball, the iFB tucks into central midfield; when the team loses it, they step out to defend the wing.

Joachim Löw's Germany used the role at the 2018 World Cup. Guardiola has used it since 2020; Arteta, Klopp and Inzaghi have all adopted it. The point is to crowd central areas and let the wingers play one-on-one.

Why it works

It numbers up the midfield. A 4-3-3 with two iFBs effectively plays 2-3-5 in possession. The downside: a long switch to the touchline can isolate the wing-back high up the pitch.

Watch

Oleksandr Zinchenko at Arsenal (2022–24) and João Cancelo at Manchester City (2020–23) are the textbook iFBs — touch-maps show they spend ~45% of possession minutes in the central half-spaces, not on the touchline.

Stat
