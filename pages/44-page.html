Midfielder archetypes

A midfield is not one position but a spectrum of roles, each defined by where the player starts, the spaces they cover, and what they do in the final third. The old 4-4-2 had two midfielders; the modern 4-3-3 names five distinct roles.

The numbered system — 6, 8, 10 — has Italian football roots and is now universal. They correspond to:

The 6. The defensive midfielder. Sits in front of the centre-backs, screens, wins the ball, recycles possession. The role of a regista when the 6 is also the team’s deep playmaker.

The 8. The central midfielder. Two sub-roles: the box-to-box (end-to-end, arrives late in the box) and the mezzala (half-space operator, drifts wide to create). Different jobs, same shirt number.

The 10. The attacking midfielder. Plays between the lines, makes the final pass, scores from deep positions. The role of trequartista in Italian tradition.

Modern trend

Since 2018, the inverted full-back has done much of the role traditionally played by the 8 — pushing into midfield while the 8 pushes into the 10’s space. The midfield roles shrink year by year as the tactics evolve around them.
