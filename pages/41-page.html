Section III

Goalkeeper roles

A goalkeeper is a position; the goalkeeper’s role is a choice. Modern football distinguishes at least four GK archetypes — defined by where they start, how far they come off their line, and what they do with the ball at their feet.

The trend across the last decade is clear: the modern keeper starts higher and passes shorter. Of the 16 keepers at the 2022 World Cup, every one was expected to play short under pressure. The traditional line-hugger is essentially extinct.

On the diagram

Four positions inside or just outside the penalty area, each labelled by dotted leader to a chip on the right. The deeper the dot sits, the more reactive the GK; the higher, the more progressive.

The four goalkeeper roles
GK 1–4

Modern goalkeepers are not interchangeable. Four archetypes, defined by how high they play and what they do with their feet.

Shot-stopper

Stays on or near the line. Aims to make the save the shot-stopper can make, not the save the defence expects. Pure reactions. Examples: David De Gea, Emiliano Martínez, Manuel Neuer (in his later years).

Sweeper-keeper

Plays 15–25 m off the line, behind a high defensive line. Reads the through-ball before it is played. Examples: Manuel Neuer, Alisson, Ederson (who combines this with build-out).

Aggressive / sweeper-up

Charges down one-on-ones before they happen; smothers strikers at the edge of the box. Examples: Marc-André ter Stegen, Diogo Costa, the goalkeeping tradition of the 1990s.

Distributed / build-out

A player-coach’s keeper: reads the press, plays short under pressure, hits long accurate switches. Often the highest-positioned dot on the pitch. Examples: Ederson, Ter Stegen, Aaron Ramsdale.
