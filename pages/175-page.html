Section XI · Fan culture & history

Shirt numbers history

WHEN A NUMBER BECAME A NAME

Numbers began as positional labels: 1 for the goalkeeper, 2–5 for defenders, 6–8 for midfielders, and 9–11 for attackers. Modern squad numbering turned them into personal brands.

From position to personality

  • Traditional 1–11: the number followed the starting position, not the individual.
  • Permanent squads: from the 1990s, many leagues let players keep a number across a season.
  • Iconic choices: 7, 9, and 10 became associated with roles, expectations, and famous predecessors.
  • Modern flexibility: regulations differ, but names, shirt sales, and player identity now matter as much as shape.

Numbers still speak tactically

A “6” may suggest a holding midfielder, a “9” a striker, and a “10” a creator—but modern positions are fluid. The number is a clue, not a formation.

Four numbers, four expectations

Supporters read numbers as a compact football language. A player can change role while keeping the same number, but the old associations never disappear completely.
